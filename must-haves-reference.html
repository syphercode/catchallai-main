A Field Reference for Building Enterprise Software

The must-haves, and why each one earns its place.

Every must-have on this list answers a real question: what breaks if this isn't here? Features without that justification are noise. Use this as a reference when scoping, evaluating, or building enterprise applications — across customer-facing tools, people systems, governance, and operations.

Customer-Facing Systems

The systems that find, win, and serve customers — and the analytics layer that proves any of it is working.

01 / CRM

Customer Relationship Management

The single record of every customer interaction across sales, marketing, and support — so the company stops repeating itself to its own customers.

Data Foundation
01Account, contact & lead model
Leads convert into contacts and accounts without orphaning history. Dedup at import and on entry. The single thing CRMs get wrong most often.
02Custom fields and objects
Every business has data the schema didn't anticipate. If you can't add fields without a developer, the CRM ages badly.
03Relationship modeling
Parent/child accounts, partner/reseller links, contact-to-contact connections. Real businesses are networks, not flat lists.
Sales Workflow
04Configurable pipeline
Stages, probabilities, required fields per stage. Hardcoded pipelines frustrate every team within six months.
05Activity tracking
Calls, emails, meetings, tasks tied to multiple records. Without this, "what's the status" becomes archaeology.
06Forecasting
Weighted pipeline, commit/best-case/worst-case categories, rep and team rollups. The number the CFO actually wants.
07Quoting / CPQ basics
Even simple line-item quotes with PDF output beat the Excel-then-email pattern that loses revenue to slow turnaround.
Marketing & Service
08Lead capture & routing
Web forms, list imports, automatic assignment. Manual lead routing is where promising leads die.
09Campaign tracking
Source attribution that reveals which channels produce revenue, not just leads.
10Cases / tickets
Linked to accounts and contacts, with SLAs, queues, escalation. The customer's history with you is one history.
11Customer history view
Every interaction across sales, marketing, support visible on one timeline. This is the actual point of a CRM.
Cross-Cutting Essentials
12Email & calendar integration
Two-way, with auto-logging. Without this, manual logging decays and the CRM becomes a fiction.
13Workflow automation
Triggers, field updates, task creation, alerts. The hours saved compound; the consistency is what scales.
14Field-level audit log
Who changed what, when. Required for SOX, HIPAA, ITAR-adjacent environments — and good hygiene anywhere.
15SSO & MFA
Non-negotiable. The CRM holds the keys to every customer relationship the company has.
16Open API + webhooks
Without these, every adjacent system (ERP, marketing, support, BI) becomes a manual export problem.
The Trap
CRMs fail not from missing features but from over-customization and poor adoption. A CRM that captures 80% of activity is infinitely more valuable than one with perfect schema and 20% adoption. Discipline beats configuration.
The Honest Test
  • Can a rep find every prior interaction with a customer in under a minute, or does it take three tabs?
  • Does the data the system holds match what reps actually believe about their deals?
  • Would the company miss this CRM if it disappeared, or just be relieved?
02 / SALES

Sales Application

The rep's daily cockpit — narrower than a CRM, judged on whether it makes the rep faster and more effective at closing deals, not on whether it stores everything.

Pipeline & Deal Management
01Clean pipeline view
Kanban or list, drag-to-update stages. This is the screen reps live in — bad pipeline UX = unused tool.
02Deal record at a glance
Account, contacts, value, stage, close date, next step, last activity in one place. No clicking through five tabs to see if a deal is alive.
03Stage gates
Required fields or checklists per stage so deals can't advance on vibes. Tied to a sales methodology if you have one.
04Forecasting with history
Weighted pipeline plus rep commits, with history so forecast accuracy improves. Without history, "we'll hit it" is faith.
Activity & Follow-Through
05"Next step" as first-class field
Not buried in notes. The single best leading indicator of deal health.
06Tasks & reminders front and center
Overdue items surface on the home screen. Sales lives or dies on follow-through; nothing decays in a list nobody opens.
07Auto-captured activity
Manual logging always decays. The app should capture as much email/call/meeting context as it can without rep effort.
08Cadences / sequences
Multi-step touch plans queued daily. Removes "who do I contact today" as a question reps have to answer themselves.
Communication & Productivity
09Email integration with templates
Tracking (opens, clicks, replies), send-later. Reps should never leave the app to email a prospect.
10Click-to-call & logging
Recording and transcription where compliance allows. Calls are evidence; un-logged calls are amnesia.
11Calendar integration with booking links
Prospects self-schedule. Removes the email-tennis that loses deals to slower competitors.
12Mobile that's actually usable
Reps log activity from cars, airports, hallways. Voice-to-text notes are huge here. Shrunken desktop UI = abandoned tool.
Insight & Close
13Deal health signals
Stalled deals, missing next steps, slipping close dates surfaced automatically — not buried in a report nobody runs.
14Win/loss tracking
Reason codes and notes — fuel for coaching and product feedback. Without this, the same losses repeat indefinitely.
15Quote/proposal generation
Branded PDFs from deal data with e-signature. Deals close in the app instead of in email threads.
16Order handoff to fulfillment
No rekeying into ERP. The handoff is where deal data goes to die in most organizations.
The Trap
A sales app fails when it becomes a reporting tool for management rather than a working tool for reps. Every required field needs to earn its place. If reps see the app as something they fill out for the boss, adoption craters and the data becomes worthless.
The Honest Test
  • Does this feature help the rep close faster, or know what to do next? If neither, it belongs in the CRM.
  • Would your top rep voluntarily use this app on a Friday afternoon to find their next move?
  • When forecasts miss, can you point to which deals slipped and why — or just to the gap?
03 / BD

Business Development

A workflow tool for opportunity development and follow-through, not a feature-complete CRM. The differentiator is what happens before the deal becomes a deal.

Account & Opportunity Model
01Flexible account + contact model
Many contacts per account, contacts that can move accounts without losing history, relationships captured (decision-maker vs. influencer). Most BD pain comes from rigid data models.
02Customizable pipeline
Stages, probabilities, expected-close. Every BD team defines its funnel differently — hardcoding it always frustrates someone.
03Activity logging tied to opps
"Next step" and "next step date" are first-class fields, not buried in notes. The leading indicator of opportunity health.
Discipline & Workflow
04Tasks & follow-up reminders
BD lives or dies on follow-through. If overdue items don't surface, people run BD from a sticky note.
05Search across everything
Full-text across accounts, contacts, notes, attached docs. Underrated until you cross a few thousand records.
06Reporting basics out of the box
Pipeline by stage, weighted forecast, activity per BD rep, win/loss reasons, lead-source attribution. Common questions, common answers.
Communication & Documents
07Two-way email integration
Auto-log inbound/outbound to the right record. Without this, logging decays fast and the CRM lies.
08Calendar integration
Meetings link to contacts and opportunities automatically — context preserved without manual effort.
09Documents at every level
Proposals, NDAs, spec sheets, meeting notes — attachable to account, contact, or opportunity. One source of truth per relationship.
Governance
10Audit log & change history
Who edited what, when. Catches data-quality drift; non-negotiable in regulated environments.
11Role-based permissions
Not everyone should see every deal value or every contact. Sensitive deals need sensitive access.
12Clean import/export & API
Trade-show lists, broker pulls, marketing integrations. If data is hard to get in and out, the app becomes a silo.
13Mobile-friendly UI
BD folks aren't always at a desk. Even responsive web is fine, but the workflow has to function from a phone.
Aerospace / Defense Extras
14Export-control flags
ITAR/EAR markers on accounts and contacts, denied-party screening status, foreign-person indicators. Compliance built into the workflow, not bolted on.
15Capture management workflow
Pre-RFP phase has its own discipline distinct from the deal pipeline. Treating capture as a stage misses the point.
16Teaming-partner tracking
Prime/sub relationships are first-class records, not buried in notes. Teaming is half the work in this market.
The Trap
Building a "feature-complete CRM" instead of a BD tool. The differentiator isn't the contact-fields list — it's workflow around opportunity development and follow-through.
The Honest Test
  • For any opportunity, can you tell what the next move is and who's making it?
  • Does the system reflect how your BD team actually works, or how the vendor thinks they should?
04 / SOCIAL

Social Media Management

Four products glued together — calendar, content studio, account hub, and listening tool. The win is the unified record of every customer touchpoint, not the size of the feature list.

Account Management
01Centralized account connection
OAuth refresh handled gracefully. Token expiration is the #1 silent failure in social media apps — posts fail at 2am with no warning.
02Brand/workspace separation
Multiple companies or clients without cross-contamination. Each workspace owns its accounts, calendar, library, team.
03Role-based permissions
Admin, editor, contributor, approver, viewer. Critical the moment more than one person touches the accounts.
04Account health view
Connection status, posting limits, API quota. Silent failures destroy trust; visible health prevents them.
Calendar & Scheduling
05Unified calendar view
Color-coded by account/brand/campaign, drag-to-reschedule. The home screen for most users.
06Time zone handling that doesn't lie
Schedule in audience time zone, view in user time zone, store in UTC. Sounds obvious; almost nobody gets it right.
07Bulk scheduling & queues
CSV import, recurring posts, time slots that auto-fill from a content backlog. Removes manual scheduling tax.
08Campaign grouping
A product launch's twenty posts across five accounts shows as one coherent thing, not twenty disconnected items.
Content Creation
09Composer with per-platform previews
Written once, tailored per platform. Show what each will actually look like before publishing — character limits, formatting, hashtag conventions.
10Media library with rights tracking
Central asset store, tagging, search, version history, license expiration. No more "where's that hero image" in Slack.
11Approval workflows
Drafts route to reviewers, comments inline, version history, locked-once-approved. Non-negotiable for regulated industries.
12UTM builder & link shortening
Every link trackable without manual URL surgery. The link IS the analytics.
Listening & Engagement
13Unified inbox
Comments, DMs, mentions, tags from all accounts in one stream. Assignable, with status and notes — so nothing falls through.
14Brand mention tracking
Beyond your own tagged mentions — keyword monitoring across public posts, with sentiment classification.
15Crisis mode
Pause all scheduled posts across all accounts with one click. Quietly essential when something blows up.
16Alert rules
Mention spikes, negative sentiment clusters, high-follower mentions — routed to email/Slack/SMS so PR doesn't find out from Twitter trending.
Reliability & Governance
17Reliable scheduled posting
Retry logic, clear failure notifications. The single most important technical job of the app.
18SSO, MFA, audit log
You're holding the keys to every brand account a company owns. Treat it that way.
19Two-person rule for high-risk accounts
A post can't go live without a second approver. Saves careers when something looks wrong at 11pm.
The Trap
"We built a scheduler and bolted listening on" — where the two halves don't share data, so a mention and a scheduled reply about that mention live in different worlds. The win is the unified record: one customer, all their interactions visible in one place.
The Honest Test
  • When a customer DMs you, can you see every prior interaction with them across every channel?
  • When a scheduled post fails, who finds out — your team, or the marketing director's boss?
05 / ANALYTICS

Web Analytics

Judged on one thing: does it answer the questions stakeholders actually ask, fast and accurately. Pretty dashboards with bad data are worse than plain numbers that are right.

Data Collection
01Reliable async tag
Loads asynchronously, doesn't block rendering, degrades gracefully on failure. Page weight matters — keep it small.
02Server-side collection option
First-party data that survives ad blockers, ITP, and cookie restrictions. Increasingly table stakes, not optional.
03Event-based model
Pageviews are one event type. Clicks, form submits, scrolls, video plays, custom business events all live in the same model.
04Tag manager support
Non-developers deploy events without a release cycle. Otherwise every measurement question becomes an engineering ticket.
Identity & Sessions
05User identification
Anonymous ID by default, attach known user ID on login. Pre-login and post-login activity stitch into one user.
06Cross-device stitching
When a user logs in on multiple devices, the journey is one journey — not three disconnected ones.
Reporting & Analysis
07Custom report builder
Drag dimensions and metrics, save, share. Without this, every question becomes a ticket to the analytics team.
08Funnel analysis
Multi-step path, drop-off rates, segmentable. Where the conversion problem actually is, not where you think it is.
09Cohort analysis
Retention curves by acquisition week/month/source. The clearest view of whether the product is getting stickier.
10Attribution modeling
First-touch, last-touch, linear, data-driven. Don't lie about which is "right" — let users compare.
11Segmentation
Ad-hoc filters and saved segments. Compare cohorts side by side without rebuilding the report each time.
Quality & Trust
12Bot & spam filtering
Bad data poisons every conversation. Known-bot exclusion plus customizable rules.
13Anomaly detection
Alert when traffic, conversion, or revenue swings beyond normal. Catches both real events and broken tracking.
14Sampling transparency
If results are sampled, say so prominently. Hidden sampling is how vendors lie politely.
15Annotations
Mark deploys, campaigns, outages on the timeline. So six months later, anomalies have context.
Privacy & Integration
16Consent management integration
Events suppress or anonymize based on consent state. The compliance baseline, not a nice-to-have.
17PII detection & redaction
Most compliance failures are accidents — PII in URLs, in event params. Auto-detection beats manual policy.
18Raw data export
BigQuery, Snowflake, S3. The serious analyses always end up in SQL eventually — if you can't get the raw data out, you're stuck with the vendor's interpretation.
19Data residency options
EU storage, US storage. Increasingly required for enterprise sales — not a nice-to-have, a procurement gate.
The Trap
Two failure modes. Tracking everything and analyzing nothing — a 200-event taxonomy nobody can navigate. And pretty dashboards with bad data — validation and debugging matter more than another visualization type.
The Honest Test
  • When a stakeholder asks "why did conversions drop yesterday," can someone answer in 15 minutes with confidence?
  • If your analytics tool disappeared today, what's the first metric people would actually miss?
06 / SEO

SEO & Search Visibility

Find what to fix, prove the work is moving the needle, and get the right things in front of engineering, content, and execs in language each can act on.

Keyword & Rank Tracking
01Keyword database with intent
Volume, trend, difficulty, intent classification, SERP feature presence. The basis for prioritization that isn't guessing.
02Daily rank tracking by location/device
Country, state, city for local; desktop and mobile separately. Historical depth — 90 days isn't enough.
03SERP feature tracking
When you own the featured snippet or PAA — and when you lose it. Clicks are increasingly in the features, not the blue links.
04Cannibalization detection
Multiple URLs ranking for the same query split authority. Visible cannibalization is fixable; invisible cannibalization isn't.
Technical SEO
05JS-rendering crawler
Respects robots.txt, handles modern frameworks, scales to large sites. Without JS rendering, you see what users see — but not what Google sees.
06Issue prioritization
Not "1,200 problems" but "these 8 affect 80% of revenue templates." Severity × scope × business impact, not raw count.
07Log file analysis
What Googlebot is actually crawling vs. what you want crawled. The most underused diagnostic in SEO.
08Core Web Vitals at template level
LCP, INP, CLS by template, not just sample URLs. Field data plus lab data — both, not either.
09Indexation analysis
What's indexed vs. not, with reasons. Index bloat dilutes authority; missing indexation hides revenue.
Content & Backlinks
10Content decay detection
Pages losing traffic, with likely causes — algorithm update, freshness, lost links, competitor takeover. Refresh decisions made on data, not feelings.
11Content gap analysis
Keywords competitors rank for and you don't, prioritized by value. The to-do list for content strategy.
12Link gap analysis
Sites linking to competitors but not you. Targeted, achievable outreach beats spray-and-pray.
13Internal linking analysis
Anchor text distribution, link depth, money-page authority flow. The free SEO lever most teams ignore.
AI Search Era
14AI Overview tracking
When you're cited, when competitors are cited, what queries trigger AI answers. The new top-of-funnel reality.
15LLM citation tracking
Visibility in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini for relevant queries. Methodologies are imperfect; the tool should be honest about that.
16AI crawler analytics
GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Google-Extended visibility and control. You can't optimize for what you can't see.
Reporting & Workflow
17Algorithm update tracking
Confirmed and likely updates overlaid on your performance. Anomalies have explanations; explanations have evidence.
18Issue tracking with project mgmt export
Findings turn into tickets exportable to Jira/Asana — not stuck in the SEO tool nobody else opens.
19Revenue attribution
Connecting organic search to pipeline and revenue. The gap between rankings and revenue is where SEO programs lose budget battles.
20GSC reconciliation honesty
When tool numbers differ from Search Console, the tool should explain why — not pretend its number is right.
The Trap
Vanity dashboards disconnected from revenue. The tool tracks 50,000 keywords, shows beautiful upward trends, and the CFO still kills the budget because nobody can answer "how much did SEO contribute to pipeline." Tracking the wrong things at high resolution is worse than tracking the right things imperfectly.
The Honest Test
  • Can the SEO team produce a one-page report tying organic search to pipeline that survives CFO scrutiny?
  • When a ranking drops, can someone diagnose root cause in an hour?
  • When 1,000 issues are found, can the team say "these 5 are this quarter's priorities" — and be approximately right?

People & Culture Systems

Hiring the right people, holding the system of record, and building the cultural fabric that makes employees stay.

07 / TALENT

Talent Acquisition

Find the right people, move them through a process that doesn't lose or insult them, produce hires that perform — and generate the evidence that the process was fair.

Requisitions & Sourcing
01Requisition workflow with HRIS link
Tied to a budgeted position, not a free-floating headcount request. Approval routing by type and seniority — finance and HR aligned.
02Multi-channel job posting
One-click distribution to LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor, niche boards. Centralized response handling — applicants don't disappear into job-board black holes.
03Career site & portal
Branded, mobile-first, fast. Every second of load time costs candidates.
04Talent pool / database
Every prior candidate, with the right to be found again on relevant roles. Privacy controls per jurisdiction.
Application & Candidate Experience
05Mobile-first apply flow
Short, with resume parsing, optional fields actually optional. Application abandonment is the single largest leak in the funnel.
06Status visibility for candidates
Realistic timelines visible to the candidate. Silence produces Glassdoor reviews; transparency produces trust.
07Self-service scheduling
Candidates pick from interviewer availability. Removes the email-tennis that costs candidates to faster competitors.
08Decline with dignity
Personalized rejections, with timing that respects the candidate's life. Candidates remember; networks talk.
Structured Hiring
09Interview kits per role
Pre-loaded resume, JD, scorecard, prior feedback. Interviewers prepared, not improvising.
10Structured scorecards
Required ratings on must-have competencies, behavioral evidence captured. Unstructured interviews are the most reliable source of bias.
11Hidden scorecards before debrief
Submissions invisible to others until all in. Stops anchoring on the highest-status voice in the room.
12Skills assessments integrated
Coding, writing, work samples — appropriate timing in the funnel, stored as part of the record.
Offer & Onboarding Handoff
13Offer approval workflow
Approval matrix by level, comp, equity. With pay-band lookup and internal-equity check before the offer goes out.
14Pay transparency support
Posted ranges by jurisdiction, with rules engine for what's required where. Compliance built into workflow.
15E-signature integration
DocuSign or similar, with completion tracking. Executed offer back in the candidate record.
16Clean transition to HRIS
No re-keying. Candidate becomes employee with history preserved. Day-one readiness — laptop works, accounts work, manager knows.
DEI & Compliance
17Adverse impact analysis
Selection rates by protected class, four-fifths rule monitoring. With statistical honesty about small-sample noise.
18OFCCP/EEOC compliance
Required data, applicant definition, AAP support. The documentation that audit demands, generated automatically.
19AI bias auditing & disclosure
NYC Local Law 144, EU AI Act compliance. Any AI in the hiring path must be explainable, audited, and disclosed to candidates.
Quality Loop
20Quality of hire tracking
First-year retention, performance rating, time to productivity by source. Closes the loop between sourcing and outcome — without it, sourcing investment is guessing.
21Source effectiveness by quality
Not just by application volume. The cheap source that produces no hires is more expensive than the premium source that produces good ones.
The Trap
Three predictable failures: candidate experience treated as a feature instead of the product; structured hiring degrading into theater (scorecards filled out post-hoc); and metrics driving the wrong behaviors (time-to-fill becomes North Star, quality drops). The software can require the artifacts; it can't supply the discipline.
The Honest Test
  • Can a candidate get a status update without emailing a recruiter?
  • When the funnel shows adverse impact, does it lead to investigation or defensive explanations?
  • Can someone tell you the quality-of-hire of last year's class, by source and recruiter — in numbers, not anecdotes?
08 / HRIS

Human Resources Information System

The system of record for employees. Every other people-related system depends on its data being correct — garbage here is garbage everywhere.

Core Record
01Single employee record
Source of truth. Every downstream system reads from here. Two records for one person breaks every report.
02Position vs. person separation
Job codes, levels, FLSA classification kept distinct from the person filling them. Lets you handle vacancies, reorgs, and acting roles cleanly.
03Effective-dated everything
Every change has effective date, entered date, reason code. Without this you can't answer "what was true on April 1" and audits become impossible.
04Org structure with history
Reporting, dotted lines, cost centers, locations, entities — with effective dating so the org chart of any past date is reconstructible.
Lifecycle
05Onboarding workflow
Pre-boarding tasks, document collection (I-9, W-4, direct deposit), equipment provisioning, day-one checklist. The handoff that determines first-week experience.
06Job change workflows
Promotions, transfers, pay changes — with approvals and effective dating. Casual changes corrupt the record.
07Leave of absence tracking
FMLA, parental, medical, military — eligibility calculations, return-to-work, disability admin integration. Where compliance failures live.
08Offboarding automation
Final pay, equipment return, access revocation triggers, COBRA, exit interview. Same-day access termination is the security baseline.
Compensation & Payroll
09Comp structure with full history
Salary, hourly, components separately tracked. The data feeding pay equity analysis and merit cycles.
10Compensation planning
Merit cycles with budgets, manager proposals, approval routing. Bulk execution with effective dates — not 500 individual change requests.
11Native or tightly-integrated payroll
Gross-to-net, multi-state, multi-country, year-end forms. The HRIS-payroll seam is where data goes to die.
12Pay equity analysis
Statistical tools that don't lie about confidence. Catches the disparities that become lawsuits.
Time, Benefits & Talent
13PTO accruals with policy variations
Eligibility rules, carryover, cash-out — by country, jurisdiction, tenure. Get this wrong and you owe money or get sued.
14Benefits open enrollment
Annual cycle with decision support, plan comparison, dependent verification. Carrier EDI feeds — manual carrier enrollment is where data dies.
15Performance review cycles
Goals, check-ins, formal reviews, calibration. Configurable to whatever methodology HR has chosen this year.
16Required training tracking
Compliance courses, certification expirations. Without this, regulators ask and you can't answer.
Self-Service & Reporting
17Employee & manager self-service
Update address, view paystubs, request time off, approve team changes. Otherwise HR runs on email and Slack tickets.
18HR case management
Tickets with SLAs, knowledge base, audit trail. Stops HR from being a black hole for employee questions.
19Field-level audit log
Every change, with attribution and timestamps. Mandatory for SOX; sensible everywhere.
20Custom reports without tickets
Non-admins build their own. Headcount, turnover, time-to-fill, comp, diversity — answerable without filing a request.
Security & Integration
21Field-level access controls
SSN, comp, performance, medical info each gated separately. The data sensitivity demands it.
22Identity provider integration
SCIM provisioning so employee changes flow into application access automatically. Joiners-movers-leavers handled.
23API for the surrounding stack
ATS, LMS, performance, payroll, benefits, identity, security, T&E. The HRIS feeds 15+ adjacent tools.
24Sandbox environment
HRIS changes break payroll if untested. Non-negotiable for any serious deployment.
The Trap
Three failures: treating it as a payroll tool with HR bolted on (or vice versa); over-customizing until upgrades break; deploying without owning data quality. The cleanest schema produces wrong reports if managers don't enter changes correctly and on time.
The Honest Test
  • When finance asks "headcount as of last quarter, by cost center, FT vs. PT, with avg tenure and salary" — can someone answer in 10 minutes from a single source?
  • If that question kicks off a 3-day reconciliation between HR/payroll/finance, the system isn't actually the system of record yet.
09 / CULTURE

Culture & Recognition

Make appreciation visible, frequent, and tied to what the company actually values — without becoming gamified surveillance theater that employees see through within a quarter.

Recognition Mechanics
01Peer-to-peer as foundation
Anyone recognizes anyone, not just top-down. Top-down-only is the most reliable way to make a program feel performative.
02Public feed with reactions
Visibility is what produces social reinforcement. Private-only is a thank-you note, not a culture lever.
03Tagged to company values
Each recognition pinned to an actual company value. The mechanism that turns recognition into culture-shaping rather than a generic kudos board.
04Specificity prompts
"What did they do, what was the impact, which value." Quality of recognition matters more than volume.
Rewards & Incentives
05Points-based with budgets
Manager and peer allocations refreshed monthly. Without budgets, points either inflate to meaninglessness or deflate to nothing.
06Reward catalog with regional honesty
Gift cards, merchandise, charitable donations, custom rewards — with regional availability handled honestly.
07Service awards / milestones
Anniversary recognition at meaningful intervals. "No one acknowledged my 5-year" pain is widespread and entirely preventable.
08Tax handling baked in
Taxable rewards properly reported (W-2 imputation, P11D). Without this, payroll and recognition reconcile by hand quarterly.
Listening & Feedback
09Continuous feedback (separate from reviews)
Peer, upward, 360 — on demand, developmental in framing. Feedback shouldn't be a once-a-year ceremony.
10Pulse surveys with rotating banks
Short, frequent, with rotating questions so the same questions don't get the same answers from fatigue.
11Lifecycle surveys
Onboarding (30/60/90), exit, post-promotion, post-restructure. Feedback at the moments when it's most diagnostic.
12Action planning & follow-through
Manager dashboards, action plans, communication back to teams. Surveys without action are worse than no surveys — they signal the company asks but doesn't listen.
Equity & Trust
13Recognition equity analysis
Does recognition flow disproportionately to certain demographics, levels, in-groups? The most important DEI lens on recognition data.
14Real anonymity protection
Small-team aggregation rules, no demographic combinations that re-identify. The technical implementation must match the marketing message.
15Wall between development & evaluation data
Recognition and feedback used for development must not flow into performance evaluation. Once employees suspect leak, every signal degrades.
Connection & Communication
16Rich employee directory
Pronouns, time zone, working style, fun facts. Humanizes colleagues — particularly important for distributed teams.
17Q&A platform for all-hands
Upvoting so most-asked questions surface. Anonymous-asking option. Otherwise the same five voices dominate.
18ERG & community group support
Operational backbone — group spaces, events, budgets, membership. Without it, ERGs exist on paper but don't function.
19Chat & HRIS integration
Recognition flows in chat where work conversation actually happens. SCIM provisioning so the employee data is always current.
The Trap
Three failures: performative theater employees see through (generic values, hollow "great job!" recognitions, executives who never recognize); transactional points economy where employees calculate point values and recognition becomes about the reward; and over-instrumented surveillance (sentiment analysis, network analysis, manager dashboards used in performance decisions). The same data feels collaborative for development and Orwellian for evaluation.
The Honest Test
  • Would employees still use the recognition system if there were no points or rewards?
  • When the survey identifies a problem, can someone point to specific actions and outcomes a year later?
  • If you removed the app for a month, would the culture suffer or just feel quieter?

Governance Systems

Legal, compliance, and finance — the systems that keep the company out of trouble and produce numbers that survive scrutiny.

10 / LEGAL

Corporate Legal

Manage risk, contracts, and legal work across the company without being so heavy that the business routes around it. The win is fewer surprises, not more process.

Matters & Intake
01Matter as central object
Type, status, owner, jurisdiction, related entities. Everything else hangs off matters. Without this, legal work is email threads.
02Structured intake workflow
Triage rules that route to the right team. Prevents the "drive-by email to the GC" pattern that bypasses the system entirely.
03Privilege flagging
Privileged work product technically distinguishable, not just labeled. Required for defending privilege claims in litigation.
Contract Lifecycle (CLM)
04Contract repository with metadata
Full-text search, OCR, structured fields (counterparty, value, term, renewal, jurisdiction). Without this, contracts are findable only by the person who signed them.
05Clause library & playbook
Pre-approved language with fallback positions. The single biggest accelerator for repetitive contract work.
06Self-serve contracts for the business
NDAs, simple SOWs, vendor agreements generated with guardrails. Legal isn't the bottleneck on routine paper.
07Approval workflows by risk
Routing on contract type, value, deviation from playbook. Conditional approvers (security, finance, export control, tax).
08Obligation extraction & tracking
Renewal dates, notice periods, audit rights, MFN, change-of-control. The post-signature life of a contract is where value leaks.
Entities & IP
09Entity register with org chart
Subsidiaries, jurisdictions, officers, directors, with effective dating. Reconstruct corporate structure as of any past date.
10Annual filing tracking
Franchise tax, registered agent, with deadlines and proof of filing. Missed filings are an expensive form of carelessness.
11IP register & docketing
Patents, trademarks, domains with key dates. The deadline calendar that keeps rights from lapsing.
Litigation & Outside Counsel
12Legal hold management
Scope, custodians, hold notices with acknowledgment, periodic reissue. Required for any litigation-prone organization.
13E-billing with guideline enforcement
LEDES ingestion, automated rejection of block billing and rate violations. Where legal departments justify their existence to finance.
14Matter budgets with alerts
Budget vs. actual at the matter level, with threshold alerts. Without this, surprises arrive as invoices.
Compliance & Knowledge
15Policy library with attestation
Version control, ownership, review cadence, employee acknowledgment tracking. Required for regulated environments.
16Investigation case management
Restricted access by case, hotline integration, investigation workflow. Most cases must be invisible to most of the legal team.
17Word/Outlook integration
Attorneys live in those tools. An app that forces them out will be abandoned.
18AI-assisted contract review
Clause extraction, risk flagging, comparison against playbook. Single biggest productivity lever — but must never let users skip review on high-stakes work.
Aerospace / Defense Extras
19ITAR/EAR matter classification
Foreign-person access controls on documents and matters. Empowered-official approval workflows. License/TAA/MLA tracking.
20Technology Control Plan (TCP) management
TCP integration mapping projects to controlled programs. Deemed-export logging. The compliance backbone for export-controlled work.
The Trap
Two failures: becoming a filing cabinet (everything stored, nothing actionable, deadlines missed); or becoming a process bottleneck (every paper requires workflow, business sends it from personal Gmail instead). Automation of the routine is what frees attention for genuinely judgment-requiring work.
The Honest Test
  • Does the business voluntarily route work through the system because it's faster than going around it?
  • If legal has to police usage, the design has already lost.
11 / COMPLIANCE

Corporate Compliance

Make sure the company knows its obligations, can prove it's meeting them, and can detect and respond when it isn't. Documentation isn't compliance; behavior is.

Program Foundation
01Regulatory & obligation register
Every law, regulation, standard, internal policy applicable to the company. The spine of the whole program; without it, compliance is vibes.
02Control framework with cross-walk
Many-to-many mapping (one control covers multiple obligations). When ISO 27001 maps to NIST maps to SOC 2, evidence collected once satisfies all three.
03Risk register with treatments
Risks scored, owned, with mitigation plans linked to controls. The map between what could go wrong and what's being done about it.
Policy & Training
04Policy library with mapping
Linked to controls and obligations. When a regulation changes, you can find every policy that needs updating.
05Policy attestation
Assigned by role, location, system access, with acknowledgment tracking. Evidence retention for audit.
06Required training tracking
Code of conduct, anti-bribery, export controls, privacy, with completion deadlines and recertification. Where compliance failures live in audits.
07Conflict-of-interest disclosures
Annual and event-driven, with review workflow. The annual ritual that catches the issues nobody volunteered.
Controls & Testing
08Control library & ownership
Description, frequency, type, automation level, key vs. non-key. The controls that satisfy obligations, mapped to evidence.
09Continuous control monitoring
Automated checks, exceptions routed for review. Detects drift before audit catches it.
10Issue / findings management
Severity, owner, due date, evidence of closure. Aging issues are the single best predictor of a program in trouble.
Third-Party Risk
11Vendor inventory with criticality
Data access, system access, geographic footprint, inherent risk rating. Know who you depend on before they fail you.
12Due diligence workflow
Questionnaires, document collection (SOC 2, insurance), screening (sanctions, adverse media), risk scoring.
13Continuous monitoring
Re-screening for sanctions hits, financial health changes, breach disclosures. Vendors change after onboarding.
Investigations & Disclosure
14Whistleblower hotline integration
Anonymous and identified intake, with legally required independence from management.
15Case management with restricted access
Conflict screening at intake, restricted to investigation team. Most cases shouldn't be visible to most of compliance.
16Privacy / DSAR workflow
Data subject requests with intake, identity verification, fulfillment, deadline tracking. GDPR/CPRA compliance baseline.
Reporting & Audit Trail
17Tamper-evident audit trail
Field-level change history with attribution. The most important technical feature — the compliance app itself is in scope for audit.
18Executive & board dashboards
Program health at a level a board committee can consume. Top risks, open issues, training, attestations, incidents.
19External auditor portal
Request lists, evidence sharing with controlled access, status tracking. Cuts weeks off external audit cycles.
Aerospace / Defense Extras
20Empowered Official workflow
EO designation and approval routing for ITAR transactions. Required by the regulations.
21Denied/debarred party screening
DPL, SDN, Entity List, Unverified List screening at customer, vendor, employee, visitor level — with re-screening cadence.
22CMMC / NIST 800-171 management
Assessment scoring, POA&M tracking, FAR/DFARS clause flow-down. The compliance bar for federal aerospace/defense work.
23Voluntary disclosure workflow
For inadvertent violations of ITAR/EAR. The narrow window between "you found it" and "you have to disclose it."
The Trap
Two failures: becoming a documentation factory disconnected from behavior (passes audits but the underlying behavior hasn't changed); or becoming so heavy that the business routes around it (controls bypassed, exceptions granted permanently). The discipline is making compliant behavior the easy path.
The Honest Test
  • Can you answer "what's our exposure to [new regulation]" in a day, not a quarter?
  • When an issue is found, does it close with evidence, or does it age?
  • Do investigations result in remediation that prevents recurrence, or do the same root causes show up across cases over years?
12 / FINANCE

Corporate Finance

One core job: produce numbers that are correct, on time, and trusted. Everything else serves that.

General Ledger Foundation
01Multi-dimensional chart of accounts
Natural account, entity, cost center, project, product, location, intercompany. The single most consequential design decision — getting it wrong means rebuilding everything downstream.
02Multi-entity, multi-currency, multi-book
Each entity with own ledger, currency, calendar, accounting basis (GAAP, IFRS, statutory, tax) maintained in parallel.
03Effective-dated everything
Accounts, hierarchies, exchange rates, allocations. Reconstruct what was true on any past date — required for restatement and audit.
04JE workflow with separation of duties
Preparer, reviewer, approver, with documentation and threshold-based routing. Prevents the most common SOX deficiencies.
Procure-to-Pay
05Vendor master with controls
Tax IDs, banking, with workflow for adds and changes. The most common payment-fraud vector if uncontrolled.
06Three-way match with tolerances
PO, receipt, invoice automated with exception workflow. Prevents duplicate and fraudulent payments.
07Payment fraud controls
Vendor bank-change verification, dual approval, callback for high-value first-time payments. The controls that prevent BEC fraud.
Order-to-Cash
08Billing engine
Recurring, milestone, T&M, usage-based, with proration and credit/rebill. Subscription and contract complexity real businesses generate.
09Revenue recognition (ASC 606 / IFRS 15)
Performance obligations, transaction price allocation, contract modifications. Critical for any subscription or long-cycle business.
10Cash application with AI matching
Automated matching of payments to invoices, exception workflow for unapplied cash. Without it, AR aging is unreliable.
Cash & Treasury
11Bank account inventory
Every account, every entity, every signer, with periodic confirmation. The "we have how many bank accounts?" problem.
12Cash position & forecast
13-week rolling forecast with variance, by entity and currency. The runway question, answered with data.
13FX exposure & hedging
Identification, hedging, hedge accounting under ASC 815. For multi-currency businesses, where surprises live.
14Intercompany matching & elimination
Transaction-level matching, automated elimination at consolidation. The hairball in any multi-entity organization.
Close & Consolidation
15Close calendar with status
Every task, owner, dependency, deadline. Aging on overdue. The visible heartbeat of the close.
16Account reconciliation workflow
Every balance sheet account every period, auto-cert for low-risk, full workflow for material. With aging on unreconciled items.
17Consolidation engine
Multi-entity, multi-currency translation, minority interest, equity method. Audit trail of every consolidation entry.
18Flux analysis
Variance with explanation requirements above thresholds. Automates the question of "why did this account move."
FP&A
19Driver-based budgeting
Revenue from price × volume × mix, headcount from start dates and loaded costs. Beats last-year-plus-X percent.
20Rolling forecast with scenarios
Base, upside, downside cases. Quick switching for board discussions and what-if questions.
21Workforce planning integrated with HRIS
Position management, hiring plan tracking, fully-loaded cost per employee. The biggest expense planned at the right grain.
Reporting & Audit
22Drill-through to transaction
From any reported number, every time. The minute someone asks "what makes up this number" and can't find out, trust erodes.
23Continuous controls monitoring
Duplicate payments, vendor changes followed by payments, weekend transactions, just-below-threshold patterns. Detection without manual review.
24SOX evidence drawn from system data
Not manually compiled. The audit evidence is the same data the system runs on.
Aerospace / Defense / Gov Contractor Extras
25CAS compliance & indirect rates
Disclosure statement, pool/base structure, provisional vs. final rates, year-end true-up. Required for federal contracting.
26Allowable vs. unallowable cost segregation
Per FAR Part 31, automated where possible. Without segregation, ICS submission becomes a multi-month project.
27Contract-type accounting
FFP, T&M, CPFF, CPIF — each with its own revenue recognition and billing rules. DCAA-ready audit trail.
The Trap
Three failures: chart of accounts ages badly (works at $50M, breaks at $500M); close becomes performance art (heroics instead of automation); finance disconnected from operations (different teams use different numbers from different systems).
The Honest Test
  • Can someone give a confident answer to "how did Q3 go" before the books close on October 15?
  • When an auditor asks for a population of journals posted by VPs, can it be produced in 15 minutes?
  • When the CFO leaves, does the close still happen on schedule the next month?
iv

Operational Systems

The tools the company actually works in every day — where conversation happens, where work gets tracked, where knowledge is captured or lost.

13 / PM & DOCS

Project Management & Documentation

Make work visible — what's being done, by whom, by when, why — and capture the knowledge generated along the way so it survives the people who created it.

Work Modeling
01Flexible work item types
Task, subtask, milestone, issue, story, epic, risk, decision. Different teams need different schemas — one rigid task type doesn't fit them all.
02Multi-level hierarchy
Parent/child relationships so a deliverable rolls up into a phase rolls up into a portfolio. Without rollups, executive reporting is manual aggregation.
03Cross-project dependencies
Finish-to-start with lag/lead time, visualizable. Within and across projects — that's where critical paths actually live.
04Custom fields at every level
Every team tracks something the vendor didn't anticipate. If you can't add fields, the app ages badly.
05Workflow per work type
Bugs flow differently from marketing campaigns differently from legal reviews. Configurable states with required fields per transition.
Views & Planning
06Multiple view types
List, board, calendar, timeline/Gantt, table. Different users think differently — same data, multiple lenses.
07Custom saved views
Per user, shareable, with own filters and groupings. "My work today" looks different for everyone.
08Capacity / workload view
Per person, per team, across projects, showing over-allocation. Without this, planning is wishful thinking.
09Baselines & snapshots
Plan as of a point in time, so "how have we drifted since kickoff" is answerable.
Documentation Side
10Block-based real-time editor
Multi-cursor, presence, no merge conflicts. Table stakes since Google Docs normalized it.
11Version history with restore
Every revision, with diff view. Catastrophic deletions become recoverable, not career-defining.
12Templates for repeated content
Meeting notes, RFCs, postmortems, runbooks. Reduces friction on documentation that should be standard.
13Backlinks & references
Every page that links to the current page. Turns docs into a knowledge graph instead of a hierarchy.
14Federated search across both
Work items and docs, full-text, with filters. Search is what determines whether documentation is asset or archive.
Connection Layer
15First-class links between docs & work
Mention a page from a task, mention a task from a page, with previews. The two halves should feel like one product.
16Action items in docs become tasks
Meeting notes produce real follow-ups, not buried bullet points that nobody owns.
17Embed live work lists in docs
A project brief showing current milestone status without manual updates. Living documents instead of stale ones.
Memory of the Org
18Decision log
What was decided, by whom, when, with alternatives considered. Prevents "wait, why did we do it this way" six months later.
19Risk register per project
Likelihood, impact, owner, mitigation, with portfolio rollup. Risks talked about become risks managed.
20AI-assisted Q&A over content
"What's the status of project X" answered with citations, not hallucinations. The biggest emerging differentiator if done honestly.
Workflow & Integration
21No-code automation
Triggers, conditions, actions. Status changes, due dates, recurring work — without admin tickets.
22Forms for structured intake
Replaces the "send me an email" pattern that loses information and routing context.
23Deep chat & calendar integration
Create items from chat, get updates in chat, deadlines on calendar. The work flows where the conversation happens.
24Source control integration
Link commits, PRs, branches to work items. For engineering teams, indispensable for tracing what shipped to what was planned.
The Trap
Three failures: tool sprawl that fragments knowledge (Jira/Confluence/Notion/Slack with no single source); process heaviness that drives users to shortcuts; and documentation exhaustive but stale (pages from 3 years ago presented as current). "No doc" is sometimes better than "wrong doc."
The Honest Test
  • Can someone joining the team find the current state of their project, by themselves, in an hour?
  • When a project finishes, does the knowledge survive — or live in someone's personal notes?
  • When a critical person leaves, can the team continue without a multi-week archaeology project?
14 / CHAT

Internal Chat & Collaboration

Be where work conversation happens — fast enough to feel real-time, organized enough to find things later, secure enough that legal and IT don't ban it.

Core Messaging
01Channels with public/private
Public for discoverability, private for sensitive work. The most important organizational primitive — bad channel structure is unrecoverable.
02Threads
Replies that don't pollute the main channel. What separates chat that scales from chat that becomes unreadable noise.
03Mentions with notification rules
@user, @channel, @here, @group with separate notification controls. Abuse of @channel is the fastest way to make a tool hated.
04Reactions
Lightweight acknowledgment without adding messages. Reduces noise; allows acknowledgment without typing.
05Edit history visible
So people can't silently rewrite what they said. Trust requires this.
Findability & Knowledge
06Search that works
Full-text, with filters by sender, channel, date. People remember a phrase, not a date. Bad search means lost knowledge.
07Pinned messages & canvases
Lightweight wiki per channel for standing context. So new joiners don't re-ask questions.
08Saved messages / later
Bookmark something to deal with after the meeting. The "I'll get to this" pattern, made reliable.
Focus & Notifications
09Granular notification controls
Per channel, keyword, platform (desktop vs. mobile separately), time of day. Without this, users either drown or mute everything.
10Do-not-disturb & focus modes
Actually suppress notifications, scheduled for working hours and time zones. Respect for focused work.
11Status with calendar sync
Custom text/emoji with expiration. "In a meeting until 3pm" updates automatically — sets honest expectations.
12Scheduled send
Write now, deliver during recipient's working hours. Removes the "off-hours guilt" of cross-timezone teams.
Calls & Async
13Voice/video with screen share
1:1 and group, with recording, transcription. The line between "chat" and "meeting" tools is gone.
14Async voice/video messages
Short clips when typing isn't enough but a meeting is too much. Saves the calendar from low-stakes syncs.
Integration & Automation
15App / bot ecosystem
Native or marketplace integrations with Jira, GitHub, Salesforce, Workday, calendar, identity. Where chat earns its place.
16Workflow / automation builder
No-code triggers ("when 🐛 reaction, file Jira ticket"). Reduces friction on common patterns.
17SCIM provisioning from HRIS
Joiners-movers-leavers handled automatically. The day-of-termination access test most tools fail quietly.
External & Federation
18External / guest access
For vendors, customers, partners — with visual indication and explicit scope controls. Otherwise, externals end up in your main channels by accident.
19Federated channels
Shared channels between two companies, each side owning users and retention. Rapidly becoming standard.
Security & Governance
20SSO, MFA, encryption with BYOK
Customer-managed keys for regulated environments. Standard now, not optional.
21DLP for sensitive data
Detect/block PII, source code, credentials, regulated data being shared inappropriately. Per-channel policies.
22Information barriers / ethical walls
Prevent specific groups from messaging each other. Required in financial services, useful in M&A and disputes.
Compliance & Retention
23Retention policies per channel type
Min and max, with legal hold override. Legal will demand this; designed-in beats bolted-on.
24eDiscovery & export
Search across all messages with appropriate authorization, defensible export with metadata. The day legal asks for "every message between two people over six months" — the test most tools fail.
25Compliance archiving
Continuous export to Smarsh, Global Relay, Microsoft Purview for FINRA/SEC/HIPAA-regulated environments.
26Data residency
Storage region with controls extending to backups and DR. Increasingly a procurement gate.
The Trap
Two failures: channel sprawl with no governance (every project spawns five channels, half abandoned, search returns thirty stale results — unusable archaeology after eighteen months); and notification overload (when every message is urgent, no message is urgent, important communication moves back to email or starts being missed). Neither is solved by features.
The Honest Test
  • Can a new hire find the answer to a recurring question in search, or do they have to ask?
  • Does the tool make focused work easier or harder?
  • On termination day, does access disappear within the hour, including DMs and private channels?
A field reference for product evaluation, RFP scoping, and internal toolbuilding.
The features earn their place by what breaks if they're absent.
